Plant Microstructures: Fascinating Results of Self-Assembly and Inspiration for Engineering

Christoph Neinhuis, Institut für Botanik, Technische Universität Dresden

The surfaces of plants are covered by a thin extracellular membrane, the cuticle. In many species, the cuticle is covered by small wax structures in the range of serval micrometers. This wax layer represents a multifunctional interface between the plant and its environment. In recent years we intensively studied the transport of lipid substances through the cuticle as well as self-assembly processes leading to structure formation, the latest results of which will be presented.

Microstructures are important for many surface functions, especially water repellency. While in smooth surfaces contact angles reach up to 110°, roughness in the dimensions of micrometers causes super-hydrophobicity with contact angles up to 170°. In such cases, the area for adhesion of water is minimized and air is enclosed between the droplets and wax crystals. The same holds true for particles. Again, the contact area between particle and surface is minimized resulting in the adhesion of particles to the water droplets. Independant of size and chemical nature, contaminations are removed from such optimized surfaces with only a small amount of rain. Since this effect can be beautifully demonstrated on the leaves of the sacred lotus, we called it Lotus-Effect. Recent studies concentrate on the different types of surface structures in plants in relation wetting properties.

The Lotus-Effect depends only on physico-chemical properties of the leaf surfaces. Therefore, we initiated a joint project together with currently more than 12 industrial companies to transfer the Lotus-Effect into products with biomimetic self-cleaning surfaces such as facade paints, roof tiles, polymers or paints.
