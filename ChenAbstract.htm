Microengineering the Cellular Environment: Studies of How Cells Make Sense of their Surroundings

Christopher Chen, Biomedical Engineering and Oncology Departments, Johns Hopkins

The development and maintenance of tissue pattern arises from spatially localized modulation of cellular cues, including soluble cytokines, extracellular matrix, intercellular adhesion, and mechanical forces. We are investigating how the interactions between these cues cooperate mechanically and biochemically to regulate endothelial and smooth muscle cell function. We have developed microfabrication approaches originally developed in the semiconductor industry to generate spatially patterned cellular microenvironments with well-controlled variations in surface chemistry, adhesivity, and mechanics at cellular and subcellular length scales. Using these tools, we are investigating how cells interrogate and respond to their local environment. In particular, we are interested in understanding how integrins, cadherins, growth factors, and mechanical cues cooperate to control cytoskeletal structure and mechanics, cell proliferation, and differentiation. Thus, we hope to better understand the fundamental mechanisms that drive tissue organization, while building infrastructure to use microchips to integrate, manipulate, and study living cells.
